House of Representatives Preference Flows

This visualisation shows preference flows between candidates in Australian federal elections.

How preferential voting works.

Preferential voting, also known as instant-runoff voting, is a form of voting in which voters number candidates from their first to least preferred candidate.

Counting is done in rounds. In the first round, voters first preferences are counted, and the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated. In the second round, the votes for the eliminated candidate are then transferred to the candidates marked as the voters' second preferences, and the new candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated, with their votes transferred to whichever remaining candidates voters ranked as their next preference. This continues, with a single candiate being eliminated in each round, until only one candidate remains. Note however that, in practice, a candidate is guaranteed to win once they have amassed over 50% of the vote.

Understanding the visualisation.

This visualisation shows the flow of votes between rounds as a result of preferences. The vertically stacked bars represent the percentage of the overall vote candidates achieve in each round (the number in each bar), with the leftmost bar representing the first preference votes received by each candidate, and the rightmost bar representing the votes that have flowed to the final two candidates, with the winner being the one who achieves over 50%. The ribbons connecting the bars represent the flow of votes between rounds; when a candiate is eliminated, the ribbons coming out of their bar extend the bars of the candidates their votes were transferred to. The thickness of the ribbons directly correlates with the number of votes. The final two candidates are always placed on the top and bottom of the graph, respectively.

Voting data is sourced from the Australian Electoral Commission.
